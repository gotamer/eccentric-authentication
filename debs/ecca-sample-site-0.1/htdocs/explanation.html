Explanation

This site uses Eccentric Authentication. Instead of passwords it uses cryptographic certificates to create accounts.

The problems with passwords

Too easy to crack, too difficult to remember, too many of them.....

Eccentric Authentication

Eccentric Authentication (Ecca) is a protocol to replace password authentication with client certificates. By doing so we can:
  • make it easier to login and log out of web sites;
  • make it anonymous; you can sign up to site without providing any details that would tie the account to your person;
  • make communication more secure; with certificates, people can send encrypted and signed messages securely without revealing their identities. Ideal for a dating site;
  • make it possible to bootstrap other communication protocols securely.

If applied correcty, Ecca would take us out of the cryptographic bronze age into the silver age. From there we might lift ourselves into the golden age.

What Eccentric Authentication does not

Email address identity

I have my own domain that I use it as my primary email address. My email address is therefore a synonym for my real name. That, in turn implies that every account that is linked to my email addres is tied to my real world identity.

With my use of SPF to prevent spammers from abusing my email address I have less opportunity to repudiate (to lie) about the origin of a message that I might regret at a later date. I must therefore be very careful with what I write and blog about at any site that knows my email address.

I would hazard a guess that most email addresses are tied to real world identities given the pervasiveness of data tracking and profiling.

Fully validated certificates

A fully validated global CA certificate is a digital identity tied to your real world identity.

When you use a fully validated certificate, all your actions and messages are tagged with your real identity, for life.

These certificates have good use for situations where people need to identify someone by name. For example a public notary who transfers the title of a house. Or government employees who are applying laws to grant or deny requests. You need accountability, so these people have no expectation of privacy in these roles.

It doesn't mean that the notaries or civil servants don't have any right to privacy at all. On the contrary, in their personal lives, privacy is as important to them as to any one else.

Ecca doesn't replace fully validated global certificates. And it shouldn't. While Google could apply Ecca for its gmail service, it should not use it for their @google.com domain. You don't want anyone on the net to claim they work at google.

Lousy validated certificates

A Lousy Validated Certificate is a certificate that is validated on shaky grounds. Most likely, the "validation" means "control" of an email address.

It's neither meat nor fish. These certificates are good for nothing. They water the trust we can have in fully validated certificates without providing any strong identity claim nor do they proved sufficient privacy. They should go away. With Ecca, there is no need for these anymore.

Not Persona/BrowserID

Mozillas Persona/BrowserID is a Federated Identity Management system with a Lousy Validated certificate based upon email "validation". No Thanks.

Yet-Another-Account

What the Y-A-A problem actually is, is a roadblock that web site owners impose upon their visitors. As the internet is mostly anonymous in nature, site owners want to recognize return visitors.

On itself the login procedure is not too taxing but with so many sites requiring it, it becomes a burden.

When a site requires an email address for a validation email, or to send password reset messages, it ties the account to the email identity and from there to the meat space identity. It can be a high price for a comment on a blog.

Often times, too much of a burden that visitors turn away, never to return.

While there are ways to get a temporary email address at an anonymity provider, using these increases the burden.

Use of Ecca with proper browser support can solve the Y-A-A problem.

What makes Eccentric Authentication different.

Password replacement

Eccas first purpose is a system to manage an account at a site. It replaces the hassles that come with passwords. It provides the user with a method to register at the site and later log in to it. The site can recognise the user at later visits.

No third party

Users request a certificate from the site where they want to sign up to. Users do not have to deal with any third party. The site itself runs the certificate signer. Every site signs the certificates only for it's own site. With the certificate and the private key, the user can log in to the site.

No identity validations

An Ecca signer does not validate identities before signing a certificate. Ecca has only one requirement, the username must be unique at the site. The owner of the private key controls the use of the account at the site.

At different sites you create different accounts. No need to share the identity at one to another site.

Benefits

This simple set up already gives us some benefits:
  • no more weak passwords, people can't memorize strong passwords anyway;
  • no more lost passwords, browsers will handle that with the help of cloud backup and synchronisation;
  • on the server side, there is no need to store a username/password database. As soon as the certificate is signed, it contains all details needed to proces logins
  • without a password database, there are no user credentials to steal from the site. Security requirements are simpler (for simple sites). If you would gather more privacy sensitive user data from your users, your security requirements would increase accordingly.

The end of Yet-Another-Account

With Ecca certificates we can end the dreaded Yet-Another-Account roadblock.

We solve it by creating even more accounts. But we make account creation completely automatic. All it requires is one click from the user to sign up when he decides to register.

User Agent

Let me introduce the Ecca plug in. It is a browser plug in that takes care of all the crypto-details to make sure people do not have to do rsa-calculations by head. :-)

The plug in has the properties that it can:

  • recognize the site (via web-domain);
  • recognize whether a certificate is needed (a http-return code) and log in with a certificate;
  • handle the whole certificate process automatically.

How to use the Ecca plug in

When you browse to a site that requires you to create an account, the plug in (not the site) gives you two buttons:

  • "Sign up"; it asks for a username and performs the registration;
  • "Sign up anonymous": it creates a random username and signs up with it.

As soon as you have pressed the button, the browser will create the certificate request and ask the the sites' own CA to sign it. Then the plug in logs you in with the freshly signed certificate.

It takes less than a second to register and log in. And, as you didn't provide any personal details it leaves no identifiable information about you at the site. You have logged in secure and anonymous.

Notice, it is the plug in that shows the buttons, does the account creation and login, not the site. There is not even a log in page on the site.

When you later browse to the site again, the plug in recognizes the site, remembers the account and offers you to log in with the previously generated certificate. Just click on the button.

You could even tell your browser to always log you in when you navigate there.

The user can also create multiple accounts a single site. For example, at a blog site, a user can write about parenting and politics under different identities while the plugin takes care of the identity and session management. (and onion routing).

The plug in also offers a log out button that ends the authenticated session so later visits to the site are anonymous again. It also needs to delete all session data, like cookies, caches and so on.

If there is a need, there could even be a "Na-Na-Na"-button in the plug in that creates a random username, uses it for the current session and deletes the key and certificate directly afterwards. This gives you a completely new identity every time you visit the site.

Certificates are cheap. Use them aplenty.

Adoption

Right now, site owners might not be so happy to allow Ecca certificates. It gives the visitor the freedom to choose under which username to log in at any time, while staying anonymous as long as they want. But I don't give them much choice.

With a good plug in, it's easier for visitors to use certificates than to create an account and password manually. This could nugde site owners to migrate towards Ecca. It is up to them to come up with a compelling reason for their visitors to sign up with an identity and keep using it.

Why certificates

Most of the benefits I described above can also be reached with other mechanisms.

There is a compelling argument why I choose certificates:

a certificate is an identity that can be addressed

Users can point to each other on the site by their chosen usernames. In fact, someone created an account called 'cc' at this site. I don't know who did. All I have is an IP-address in my server logs. And that could be VPN-address or an onion routed address.

When he created the account, the browser genrated a private key that matches the public key in the certificate.

Anyone can fetch this certificate with: 

   wget --no-check-certificate -O cc.cert \
      'https://register.ecca.wtmnd.nl/get-certificate?nickname=cc'

From there, you can retrieve the public key from the certificate and validate messages from 'cc' or encrypt messages to send to 'cc'. It's is just like any other use of a client certificate except that you can communicate securely and anonymously with a complete stranger.

There is a difference, Ecca only provide the identity, it doesn't deal with messaging. It leaves it up to the sites to deal with that. Here's an example how a dating site could handle it.

Example, a dating site

A dating site lets people register with ecca-certificates. The certificate is used for access control to the site and to allow members to write encrypted personal messages to other dating members. A browser plug in takes care of the encryption details, the dating site takes care of delivery. When you receive a reply, the certificate tells you that it is indeed from the person you wrote to.

As the messages are encrypted with the recipients public key, the dating site can not read them. At all. As it should be.

Would a member of the site behave unappropriately, the person complaining can file this complaint at the site, revealing the signed messages and when the dating site operators agree, the offenders' account gets closed.

The account gets closed by blacklisting the certificate at the site. The certificate stays valid but the site won't allow neither log in nor message delivery, which requires log in. The only option for the offender is to open a new account and pay the membership fees again if he wants to try to contact the same person again.

As long as the complaining person did not reveal any other identities, the offender cannot reach this person anymore.

When the communication between the dating site members becomes physical, both unsubscribe at the site to make their profile unavailable and then delete their private key and certificate to hide any trail of messages.

Would the offender ever use that certificate for communication in a way that requires him to go public with his real world identity, he gives the Person-of-Interest an ability to blackmail him with his past behaviour. It's in his best interest to delete the certificate and the private key that he used in bad manners and use a new identity (most likely from another site).

Trust considerations

When you decide to hand your money to the dating site for a chance to meet people of interest, you've made a trust-decision.

You decide to join the site based upon recommendations from people you know. You wouldn't make that decision because the server certificate is signed by some certificate authority.

It's the other way around: The site-owners better makes sure their cryptography is in order or they will be hanged at the gallows of the internet for being unreliable.

And if they turn out to be crooks, you only lost some money, not your real world identity. (You did pay in bitcoin didn't you?)

Human readable cryptographic identities

I mentioned that the identity consists of two parts. One part is the username. The other part is the site name.

There is a one-to-one mapping between usernames at a site and certificates it has signed. The username is a human-readable identifier for the certificate. Given a username for a site, you can retrieve the certificate that enables you to establish a secure channel.

As each site has its own CA, there is a one-to-one mapping between site names and CA-certificates. When you have the site's CA-certificate you can validate each username.

What we need is a way that allows to trust that mapping.

There is the solution: it's called DNSSEC with DANE. With DANE, the site owner can specify the CA-certificate of his site. DNSSEC secures that statment cryptographically.

As user, you can refer to a site by its site name look up both the IP-address and the site CA certificate.

Trust, but verify

With mechanisms such as CMU Perspectives, Moxie Marlinspikes' Convergence, Chrome Certificate Pinning, Certificate Patrol plug in, Petname plug in and probably a dozen other, we have ways to detect tampering with the DNSSEC key chains from the DNS root to the site.

End run around Zooko's triangle.

Once we can reach sufficient trust in DNSSEC it allows us to create a secure mapping from site name to the sites' CA certificate. That, in turn gives us the trust in the mapping from user names to certificates signed by the site.

With that chain of trust in place, you can call your girlfriend on the phone and mention one of your ecca-identities. She types it in and her user agent retrieves the correct certificate. Using an insecure channel (the phone), you have transfered your public key safely to her computer to bootstrap a secure channel (ZRTP VOIP) where you can talk dirty, ehm, private things.

Just spell the name: cc [at] ecca.wtmnd.nl.


I do believe that once critical mass is achieved every site will accept Ecca and passwords will be a memory.
